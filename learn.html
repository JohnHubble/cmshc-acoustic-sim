1. What is RT60?

RT60 is the time (in seconds) it takes for a sound to decay by 60 decibels after the source stops. In simple terms: how long it takes for a sound to fade to silence in a room.

Clap your hands in a tiled bathroom — the sound rings for a second or two. Do the same in a carpeted bedroom — it dies almost instantly. That difference is RT60.

Sound energy decay over time. RT60 is measured from when the source stops to when the level drops 60 dB (to one-millionth of original energy).

RT60 is measured separately for each frequency band (125 Hz, 250 Hz, 500 Hz, 1000 Hz, 2000 Hz, 4000 Hz) because materials absorb different frequencies at different rates. A room might have long reverb at 125 Hz (bass takes ages to die away) but short reverb at 4000 Hz (treble gets absorbed quickly by air alone).

2. Why the target matters for sound healing

Too long (>4s)

Every gong strike piles on top of the last. The practitioner loses control — individual tones become an unintelligible wash. Participants at different positions hear completely different things.

Target (1.5–2.5s)

Each tone has time to bloom and fill the space, then fades cleanly before the next arrives. The reverb adds warmth and immersion without chaos. The practitioner can layer sounds intentionally.

Too short (<1s)

The room feels dead. Crystal bowls lose their sustain and shimmer. Gongs sound flat and lifeless. You lose the immersive, enveloping quality that makes a sphere special.

The Yang Sphere targets 1.8–2.2 seconds — slightly longer because the larger volume benefits from more spaciousness. The Yin Sphere targets 1.5–1.8 seconds — tighter for the more intimate space.

Some controlled reverb is desirable for singing bowls and gongs. The goal is not to eliminate reverberation, but to tame it from chaotic (25+ seconds untreated) to musical (around 2 seconds).

3. Calculation methods

There are multiple formulas for estimating RT60. Each makes different assumptions about the room. We use all three and compare:

Method Formula Best for Our use
Sabine RT60 = 0.161 × V / A Rooms with low, uniform absorption (concert halls, untreated spaces) Baseline — overestimates RT60 when treatment is heavy
Eyring RT60 = 0.161 × V / (−S × ln(1−ᾱ)) Rooms with moderate-to-high absorption — treated rooms Primary method — most accurate for our treated domes
Fitzroy Weighted average across surface groups Non-uniform absorption (one wall treated, others bare) Cross-check — useful since our dome top vs. walls vs. floor differ significantly

Where: V = room volume (m³), A = total absorption area (m²), S = total surface area (m²), ᾱ = average absorption coefficient.

RT60 is a ratio: large volume with little absorption = long reverb. Adding absorptive treatment increases A, bringing RT60 down.

We use Eyring as the primary method because the treated dome has significant absorption (30–50% of surfaces covered). Sabine would overestimate by 15–20% in this case. An acoustic consultant running ODEON or CATT-Acoustic would validate against Eyring as their baseline too.

4. Instrument frequency ranges

Different healing instruments produce energy at different frequencies, which interact with the room treatment differently:

Crystal Bowls
30 Hz110–660 Hz fundamental10 kHz
Pure, sustained tones with clean harmonics up to ~2 kHz. Very narrow spectral peaks make comb filtering painfully obvious — diffusers are critical for crystal bowls. Phase cancellation from the curved dome causes different tonal coloring at different listening positions unless broken up by scattering surfaces.
Tibetan Bowls
30 Hz100–500 Hz + overtones to 5 kHz10 kHz
Rich, complex overtone series with multiple simultaneous frequencies. The most forgiving instrument — broadband overtones naturally mask comb filtering. Will sound decent even with less treatment.
Gongs
30 Hz30–300 Hz fund. + dense overtones to 10 kHz+10 kHz
Deep sub-bass through full spectrum. The most demanding instrument acoustically. The 30–120 Hz energy is exactly where the sub-floor plenum bass trap matters most — without it, low-frequency buildup makes everything muddy. Also where untreated RT60 is worst (47s at 125 Hz).

The simulation runs at 500 Hz by default for the ray tracer (mid-range, representative), but the RT60 chart shows all six octave bands so you can see how each frequency is affected by the treatment.

5. Treatment zones explained

Cross-section showing treatment zones. Zone size and position vary by scenario — the simulation adjusts these to compare options.

Diffusers (blue) are mounted on the upper dome. They don't absorb much sound — instead they scatter it in many directions. This breaks up the coherent focal reflection that would otherwise create a hot spot above the participants. Think of throwing a ball at a flat wall (specular reflection — it bounces right back) versus throwing it at a pile of rocks (diffuse scattering — energy goes everywhere).

Absorbers (orange) are mounted on the mid-dome walls. They convert sound energy into heat via friction in fibrous or porous material. This is what actually reduces RT60 — each reflection loses more energy, so the sound dies away faster.

Perforated floor / plenum bass trap handles low frequencies (bass). The air cavity under the floor acts as a resonant absorber tuned to the 30–120 Hz range where gongs and large bowls produce their fundamental tones. Without this, bass builds up uncontrollably in the dome.

The simulation lets you compare different zone sizes — for example, diffusers covering the upper 40% versus 25% of the dome — to find the right balance between acoustic performance and material cost.

6. The focal convergence problem

This is the most important acoustic issue specific to spherical rooms.

A concave surface acts like a focusing mirror for sound. When you place a sound source inside a sphere, the curved walls reflect sound back toward a focal zone near the center of curvature. In the Yang Sphere, this focal zone is about 3.1 meters above the floor — directly above lying participants.

Left: untreated dome focuses all reflections to a single zone above participants. Right: diffusers scatter the reflections evenly across the floor, and the elevated stage shifts the source away from the focal plane.

The elevated stage (raising the source 30–50 cm above the floor) helps because it shifts the source away from the dome's center of curvature. The reflected wavefronts arrive at slightly different angles, causing the focal zone to blur and spread. Combined with diffusers, the convergence effect drops significantly.

7. How to read the simulation

Ray Trace (left panel)

Shows a 2D cross-section of the dome with acoustic rays traced from the source. Each line is a ray bouncing between dome and floor. Brighter lines = more energy remaining. With diffusers, you'll see rays scattering in many directions (good). Without treatment, rays converge tightly at the focal zone (bad).

RT60 Chart (right panel)

Bar chart showing RT60 at each frequency band. Green shaded band = target range. Bars within the green band are ideal. Red/tall bars mean that frequency takes too long to decay. Blue/short bars mean over-damped at that frequency. Watch 125 Hz especially — it's hardest to control and most important for gongs.

Floor Energy Distribution (bottom panel)

Shows how much acoustic energy arrives at each position along the floor. A flat curve = even distribution (participants hear the same thing everywhere). A sharp peak at center = focal hot spot (people near the middle get blasted, edges are quiet). Good treatment flattens this curve.
